The fine print on heat

01
Why the numbers disagree

A Scoville rating is measured in a lab, but the number on the label is marketing. Makers often quote the rating of the pepper, not the finished sauce — which is mostly vinegar, fruit, and vegetables diluting that pepper. Crops vary batch to batch, and the original Scoville test was literally a panel of humans sipping dilutions until they stopped feeling burn. When Season 25 sauces were sent for independent lab testing, most measured far below their claimed number. So we show the claim and a plausible range — the truth usually lives between them.

02
Not all heat is alike

Chili heat comes from a family of capsaicinoids, and the mix changes the experience: some hit the tip of the tongue instantly, others creep into the throat a minute later. Your own sensitivity varies too — the receptor that senses capsaicin (TRPV1) desensitizes with regular exposure, which is why chiliheads genuinely feel less. And other “spicy” foods work on different nerves entirely: black pepper’s piperine is milder, wasabi and mustard hit the nose, ginger barely registers on the same scale. SHU only measures capsaicinoids.

03
What to expect

Capsaicin tricks nerves into reporting heat — nothing is actually burning. Typical responses: burning lips and tongue, sweating, a flushed face, runny nose, watery eyes, and hiccups. At the top of this chart, add a racing heart, stomach cramping, and occasionally nausea. It peaks within a few minutes and fades as the receptors exhaust themselves. Unpleasant, temporary, and — for a healthy adult — not harmful. The pain is real; the damage is not.

04
How to put it out

Capsaicin dissolves in fat, not water. Whole milk is the proven remedy — its casein protein pulls capsaicin off the receptor; yogurt and ice cream work the same way. Sugar water or honey measurably helps. Starches (bread, rice) give modest relief. Plain water and beer mostly spread the oil around, and carbonation can make it sting more. Cold anything numbs temporarily. Failing all that: time. Fifteen minutes and the nerves stand down on their own.

Method — Claimed SHU: official Hot Ones / HEATONIST figures, or commonly cited manufacturer numbers for benchmarks. Low/High: plausible range for the finished sauce based on ingredient dilution and precedent from independent lab testing of a prior season. Best guess: midpoint informed by the pepper’s position in the ingredient list. These are informed estimates, not lab measurements; no independent test of the Season 30 pack has been published. Reference: HPLC lab testing of Season 25 sauces (Howtown, Dec 2024) found most sauces well below claimed SHU.
